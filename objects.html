Identity Cards

In 1941, as rumours of war grew louder, every civilian in Singapore was ordered to carry an identity card under new security laws. Officially, it was meant to catch spies but for many, the small card felt like a warning that war was coming closer.

Banana Notes

The Japanese-issued currency lost value overnight due to hyperinflation and a severe depreciation in the value of the banana note. This fueled a dangerous black market.

Ration Card

During the Japanese Occupation, food was scarce and strictly rationed. Locals queued for hours for rice, limited by ration cards. Many faced severe hunger, while others grew food or made substitutes to help their communities survive.

Read More

Japanese Textbookds

Schools were forced to teach Japanese using textbooks filled with new language and propaganda. These books became symbols of how daily life, and young minds, were reshaped under occupation.

Read More

Syonan Gaho Magazine

This Japanese magazine showed smiling locals to portray life under occupation as happy and prosperous. In truth, many faced hunger and fear. Such images were propaganda, hiding hardship behind an idealized facade.

Read More

Sook Ching Registration Stamp

Japanese feared Chinese resistance in Singapore. So, they ordered men to report for mass screenings called Sook Ching, meant to hunt out enemies. Many never returned. Those who survived got this stamp, a small mark that meant life.

Read More

Vaccination Certificate

To curb diseases like smallpox and typhus, the Japanese enforced compulsory vaccinations across Singapore. Civilians queued for injections and received certificates as proof. These small papers offered protection from illness but also reminded people how tightly daily life was controlled under occupation.

Read More

Japanese Purchasing Ticket

Under Japanese rule, purchasing tickets controlled what civilians could buy. This one, for a silk sari, shows how daily life blended culture, scarcity, and a barter economy.
