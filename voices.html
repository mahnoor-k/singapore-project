Echoes of Everyday Life

Beyond official documents lie the memories of ordinary Singaporeans. These voices offer glimpses into hunger, fear, hope, and resilience during the Japanese Occupation.

The Night Everything Went Silent

“Everything was silent — the shooting stopped, the shelling stopped. You drop a pin, you could hear it. The whole night we couldn’t sleep. We didn’t know what to expect the next day or what to expect at any moment.”

— Heng Chiang Ki, recalling the night of 15 February 1942

“It Was Just a Bluff”

“The British left us with the impression that Singapore was impregnable … no one thought for a moment that the Japanese would really attack. We thought that it was just a bluff.”

— Oral history interview with Lee Kip Lin, 1984

“We Thought Everything Is Lost”

“The surrender of the British was announced over the radio … We thought everything is lost. Then fear of what the Japanese, how they are going to deal with us.”

— Oral history interview with Tan Guan Chuan, 1984

Acts of Kindness in Changi Prison

“During the time that I was in prison there [Changi Prison] in Singapore, little Chinese boys would slip through and hand us a loaf of bread … Here was an anonymous person risking limb, if not life, risking very sharp injuries, in order to give a little assistance to a total stranger whom he’ll never see again.”

— Oral history interview with David Marshall, 1984

Life at Endau Settlement

“They said there would be food for us [at Endau]. In reality, there wasn’t any … You lived simply, with tree bark for walls and beds. Everything in the room was made from wood planks, the floor was muddy, and the kitchen was in a row at the back, all joined together.”

— Oral history interview with Zee Swee Fook, 1984

“People Were Actually Starving”

“People were actually starving throughout the occupation. They depend[ed] on every scanty ration supplied by the government. At the same time, they had to look for other foodstuff like tapioca and other grains, which they procured from other sources like [the] market, paying black market prices … They did all kinds of things to keep their life and soul together.”

— Oral history interview with Valuppillai s/o Pandarapillai, 1983

“Quite a Bag of Japanese Notes”

“When you wanted to buy anything you have to bring a lot of Japanese notes. I remember one towel which we had used already, we sold for $300. So it was quite a bag of Japanese notes.”

— Oral history interview with Chu Shuen Choo, 1984

“We Used to Sell Our Things”

“If your salary was, say, $100, if you have to pay $10 for an egg, you can imagine how you are going to survive? So we used to sell our things.”

— Oral history interview with Ronendra Karmakar, 1983

“Whisper Into the Wrong Ear”

“There are people who work with the Japanese news agency…. They used to hear the broadcasts from other countries. … They whisper, pass on to their trusted friends to boost up their morale. And even that, whispering must make sure that you don’t whisper into the wrong ear.”

— Oral history interview with Robert Chong, 1983

“Woke Us Up From Our Slumber”

“The Japanese liberated Malaysia and Singapore from the British. Under British rule, before the Japanese came, we were all sleeping. The Japanese invasion woke us up from our slumber and made us more determined to fight the British for our independence.”

— Oral history interview with Alias bin Osman, 1984
